# Theory of Constraints Applied to Knowledge Work

Eli Goldratt's Theory of Constraints, originally developed for manufacturing, argues that every system has exactly one binding constraint at any given time, and that optimizing non-constraints is waste. The counterintuitive prescription is to deliberately subordinate everything else to the constraint — even if that means letting non-bottleneck resources sit idle.

Most knowledge workers and organizations violate this principle systematically. They measure productivity by utilization rates across all resources, which creates pressure to keep everyone busy. But in a system with a true bottleneck, feeding work into non-bottleneck stages faster than the bottleneck can absorb it simply inflates work-in-progress inventory. In software development, this looks like a large backlog of "ready to develop" stories when the real constraint is QA or deployment.

The practical diagnostic is simple: find where work piles up. That pile is your constraint. Once identified, the five focusing steps are to exploit the constraint first (maximize its throughput with no capital investment), subordinate everything else to that exploitation, and only elevate if exploitation isn't enough.

The insight that transfers directly to consulting and strategy work is about leverage points. Organizations routinely invest in improving functions that feel slow but are not actually the binding constraint. A McKinsey engagement that recommends reorganizing the sales force when the real bottleneck is the product approval process has missed the constraint entirely.

For AI-enabled operations, the constraint is often human review and decision-making, not model inference. Automation that accelerates model outputs without addressing the human review step simply moves the pile. The right question is always: where does work wait, and why?
